    James Vaccaro

    1913–1985 — Pennsylvania

    I grew up hearing the James stories like little parables — a man who seemed made of plain things: workboots, an unhurried nod, and a steady grip. My parents would say he was the kind of person who could fix a truck with a coat hanger and a quiet swear word; he patched roofs, coaxed stubborn mules, and kept the house warmed in winters that felt endless. To me, James is the root of a family that learned to persist. Even in the smallest recollections there is a tenderness: the way he would hum while he worked, the small nick in the wooden table that still shows where he whittled a toy, and the stories you could only hear while huddled close to the stove. He taught a way of being — practical, patient, and kind in the ordinary way that lasts.

    Rose Louise Kirila

    b. 1929 — Ohio / Hungarian roots

    Rose's kitchen is the first true country I knew, though I never stood in Hungary. In every family retelling she is the woman with flour on her sleeves and hands that remembered recipes long after the ingredients were gone. My aunts would whisper about her stubborn garden where tomatoes came in despite drought, and how she could read a sky like scripture. She stitched the old country into American life with stories that tasted of paprika and patience. Growing up, I’d sit and listen as she spoke soft prayers and older relatives sang next to a radio. Rose is the voice that made our holidays smell like home — yeast and sugar and the calm steadiness of someone who kept memory alive with bowls and spoons.

    Eugene Edward Kirili

    1936–1994 — Missouri / Ohio

    Eugene's life crosses small towns and steady routines. My family always remembered him for a practical laugh and a deep loyalty to the neighborhood circle: church breakfasts, slow drives to visit kin, the small furniture he repaired for neighbors. He married Ruth and together they raised a family that knew neighbors by the sound of their cars. The stories about Eugene are not grand, but they are full of care — a hand that helped with a winter roof, a pocketful of quick advice, and a faithful presence that threaded the family together. When I think of him, I see Sunday mornings and the slow, reliable cadence of everyday care.

    Ruth Adaline Barris

    b. 1939 — Missouri

    Ruth is the neighbor who kept the phone line warm with check-ins and the church pew always found its place at family gatherings. My memories of Ruth are full of small domestic rituals: the way she folded napkins for holidays, the tea she served that made everything feel possible, and the morning calls to relatives with updates that somehow stitched days together. She raised children who learned courtesy at a kitchen table and who carried forward the gentle discipline of calling when promised. In our family stories she is warmth and steadiness, an everyday lighthouse.

    William Patton

    Northern Ireland — generation prior

    William arrived in our tales with stories of saints and small talismans: a chipped cup, a prayer-book, the kind of object that held a person's steady hand across an ocean. The family always spoke of his patience and a dry humor that could soften the hardest meeting. I picture him at the head of a long table, teaching kids to wait for dessert and to respect a good story. There was a calm authority in William — not loud, but impossible to ignore — and those rules of decency and care he handed down shaped the ways the next generations leaned into family and faith.

    Beatrice Fisher

    Ireland / Crozier notes

    Beatrice's life is stitched in the quiet ways of homemaking that feel like an old song: crisp collars in photographs, careful hair, and a pride in preserving small domestic rituals. The Crozier name surfaces in photographs and folded letters; Beatrice is the woman who knew how to make a house feel like a sanctuary. Family notes say she taught baking like a language, and those baked loaves carried memory across decades. I have always loved the idea that Beatrice saw value in the ordinary, and taught the next generations how to translate scarcity into comfort.

    Lynne Patton

    Unknown — Ireland/UK connection

    Lynne shows up in family festivals and reunion stories as someone who quietly organized the chaos of family gatherings. She was the aunt who remembered every birthday and who could be counted on to show up with a smiling face and a casserole in hand. The versions of Lynne I heard were full of warmth — an organizer, a memory-keeper, and the person who could make a weary family feel like a functioning one again. For me, Lynne is the invisible hand that smooths the edges of big family life.

    Gary Patton

    b. 1956 — Belfast → California

    Gary's stories cross the ocean: the sound of rain in Belfast, the stubborn pride of a well-steeped cup of tea, and the bracing humor that arrived with him to California. Growing up we heard of his mix of sternness and private tenderness — the man who could set strict rules and then surprise people with a cake or a fixed bike. Gary learned how to hold two homes in his heart: the old ways of Belfast and the new rhythms of California. That balance shows up in how he raised his children — with clear expectation, yes, but also an abiding loyalty that made the family feel secure.

    Daniel Patton

    b. 1992 — Woodland Hills, CA

    Daniel represents the California edge of our family tree: practical, easygoing, and quietly skilled. I remember stories of him pulling a lamp together out of spare parts and showing up for dinners that matter. He carries the patience of earlier generations but in a modern form — a willingness to tinker, to laugh, and to show up. The way he inhabits family life is less about ceremony and more about being present when it counts.

    Shannen Patton

    b. 1987 — Woodland Hills, CA

    Shannen has always been the one who keeps the calendar for the family — birthdays, dinners, holidays — and who cradles small rituals even when life is busy. She blends Patton tradition with California habits: family recipes meet long drives, and the phone call on a birthday feels like a small anchor. Married to Brian, she is the quiet hub that spins family obligations into actual gatherings, and that care is visible in the way everyone looks to her for news or comfort.

    Brian Kupfer

    b. 1981 — Ojai, CA

    Brian is the practical heartbeat of our immediate household memories: someone who can teach a skill with patience and who treats late-night pancakes as a kind of sanctuary. He worked in tooling, coached abroad, and has a way of treating ordinary moments as sacred. Married to Shannen, he built a life of quiet routines and weekend projects that felt like rituals. I think of Brian as the person who makes home feel safe — steady hands, quiet humor, and a willingness to roll up sleeves when needed.

    Robert Kupfer

    Pennsylvania — elder Kupfer

    Robert carried the family through practical care and steady presence: calls on weekends, small gatherings, and a pride when his children learned trades. He was the kind of father who valued steady employment and quiet competence, and those values rubbed off on the rest of us. Remembering him is to remember reliability — someone who did the small things that made life hold together.

    Patricia Kupfer

    Pennsylvania — elder Kupfer

    Patricia kept the family connected through rituals that felt like glue: the holiday table, a tidy guest room, and a phone always ready for a check-in. Her kindness wasn’t flashy; it was the steady work of making people feel seen. I grew up learning from the way Patricia set a place for whoever showed up and how she called to check on distant relatives as if it were the most natural thing in the world.

    Crystal Kupfer

Crystal’s presence in our family is an example of how fierce love looks in practice.

    April Kupfer

    Ojai, CA — Kupfer

    April’s life has known complications and strength in equal measure. Family memory remembers someone who fought for her children and who kept moving forward when the road narrowed. There is a toughness there — not without softness — and that combination commands respect in our stories.

    Everett Kupfer

The discipline of service translated into a gentle presence at family events: someone who listened, who showed up, and who lent perspective on days that felt fraught. His life reminds us that service shapes more than careers; it shapes how you carry yourself among kin.

    Jonathan Kupfer

    Ojai, CA — Kupfer

    Jonathan is a quieter thread in the family fabric, the step-uncle who shows up and helps without much fanfare. Stories about Jonathan are small — helping hands, a willingness to drive someone to an appointment — but those hands are the ones families rely on when plans demand extra effort.

    Nannete Dean

    Thousand Oaks, CA — Aunt

    Nannete was worldly in ways the rest of us only dimly understood when we were kids: stories of France, markets, and fig trees that tasted like summers. She carried a patient generosity and recipes that tasted of other places, and those little foreign traces made our family feel larger than its streets. Her calm presence taught me that travel can be translated into the steadiness of baked bread and the patience of a good listener.

    Mario Dean

    1985–2014 — Thousand Oaks

    Mario’s life was vivid and intense in the way that leaves a long echo. Family memories hold him as complex: brilliant, vulnerable, and alive in a way that made people both worry and laugh. He left behind stories and jokes that still surface at gatherings, small moments that carry his voice forward. Remembering Mario is bittersweet: he is warmth and a reminder to hold those we love a little closer.

    Carlo Dean

    Carlo’s story is full of contradictions — fierce loyalty, difficult chapters, and moments of deep tenderness. He’s been in and out of tough times, but family stories also record moments of care and support. Carlo’s life reminds us that people are rarely simple; they are a mix of struggle and kindness, and our family remembers both without forgetting the complexity.

    Renato Dean

To our family, Renato is a reminder that service and quiet strength often travel together.

    Demetri Dean

    Michael Dean

    b. 2008 — Thousand Oaks

    Michael — that’s you — and in the way I tell the family story, you’re the small, stubborn heart beating at the center of recent memories: long car rides, holiday mischief, and a curiosity about the old recipes and stories. You inherited pieces of different branches: a taste for Italian name-thread and the patience of the Patton and Kupfer hands. I like to think you’ll carry our stories forward in the small practical ways that matter most — the kind of memory that survives in meals shared and jokes told late at night.
